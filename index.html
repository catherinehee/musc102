Disney's Musical Representation of Non-Western Cultures

From Orientalism to Commodified Inclusion: A Critical Timeline (1953-2021)

The Walt Disney Company occupies a globally influential position within the landscape of cultural production. Its animated features, theme parks, and media conglomerates widely propagate its narratives, critically shaping worldviews of audiences. Today, Disney's works are globally consumed cultural artifacts that have been integral to entertainment for generations.

Disney is not only a storyteller, but also a commercial Western entity with market-driven imperatives. Walt Disney believed in the "terrific power of music," asserting that films would be "dragging and boring" without it; music imbues them with "life and vitality." Following this philosophy, Disney expertly leverages music to propel narratives and establish depicted identities. Yet, just as music communicates culture, music also transmits ideas and concepts. Thus, Disney's influences must be rigorously scrutinized, as the corporation prescribes ideologies beneficial to Western dominance.

In recent film releases, Disney has noticeably incorporated more accurate depictions‚ÄîColombian representation in Encanto, Polynesian collaboration in Moana, S√°mi consultation for Frozen II. However, critics question whether Disney's efforts genuinely constitute cultural appreciation or merely sophisticated forms of appropriation.

The Argument

Although Disney has replaced the stereotyped music of its earlier films with collaborative compositions of its modern releases, this methodological shift masks a continuity in power. Current inclusion strategies only serve to insulate the corporation from critique, allowing it to commodify cultural difference while maintaining strict Western control over the final product.

Central Question: Has Disney's evolution in representing non-Western cultures constituted genuine decolonization or merely sophisticated adaptation of cultural hegemony?

Through analysis of musical soundtracks, scholarly critiques, and interviews with involved musicians, this visual essay investigates how Disney's animated films' musical representation of non-Western cultures has evolved since the 1990s‚Äîand traces its roots back to the 1950s. Seven Decades, Three Methodologies

This study categorizes Disney's evolution into distinct periods based on their approach to cultural representation. What emerges is a pattern of adaptation: as each methodology faces critique, Disney evolves its methods while preserving structural control. The corporation invites "Others" into the room‚Äîbut has not handed over the baton.

Pre-Phase I

1950s

Overt Caricature

Exemplar: Peter Pan (1953) deployed crude sonic markers‚Äîdrums, chanting, invented "gibberish" like "Hana Mana Ganda"‚Äîto construct Indigenous peoples as primitive spectacle. "What Made the Red Man Red?" exemplified entertainment as explicit racist discourse, with no pretense of authenticity or respect. Complete exclusion of represented communities defined this era's logic.

Phase I

1990s-2000s

Orientalism & Sanitization

Exemplars: Aladdin (1992), Pocahontas (1995), Mulan (1998). The Renaissance Era replaced crude caricature with "aural shorthand"‚Äîpentatonic scales for "Asian," augmented seconds for "Middle Eastern." Composers deployed pre-existing colonial tropes as superficial signification. Pocahontas sanitized genocide through pop ballads; Mulan coded Eastern values as oppressive, Western as liberating. Cultural creators remained excluded from decision-making.

Phase II

2010s-2020s

Consultative Appropriation

Exemplars: Moana (2016), Frozen II (2019), Encanto (2021). Responding to criticism, Disney implemented formalized collaboration‚ÄîOceanic Cultural Trust, S√°mi verddet, Colombian consultants. Surface-level authenticity improved: Indigenous languages, traditional instruments, visual accuracy. However, consultants provide input while Western executives make final decisions. Managed inclusion extracts legitimacy without redistributing authority.

Discussion: From Orientalism to Consultative Appropriation

The comparative analysis demonstrates that while Disney's methods have evolved from exclusion to consultation, fundamental mechanisms of cultural power remain structurally intact. Phase I exemplified Said's Orientalism‚ÄîArab and Indigenous cultures constructed as exotic Others through stereotypical musical signifiers and complete exclusion from creative processes. The augmented second interval in Aladdin and the fabricated "blue corn moon" in Pocahontas represented extraction without consultation.

Phase II evolved rather than eliminated Orientalist logic. Disney now incorporates non-Western creators on terms that preserve Western authority. When Foa'i revises lyrics for "English ears" or Beck dismisses joik as "Hey Na Na music," collaboration occurs without power redistribution. The corporation transitioned from blatant Orientalism to sophisticated commodity racism: ethnic identity provides marketable "spice" while Western conventions structure the final product.

The Economic Imperative: Why Structural Change Remains Constrained

The culture industry framework reveals why structural change remains impossible under current conditions. Disney executives assume‚Äîreinforced by decades of market data‚Äîthat Western musical conventions constitute "universal" emotional language ensuring profitability, while non-Western structures represent financial risk. Economics explains the systematic "wrapping" pattern in Moana and the substitution of kulning for joik in Frozen II. These are calculated commercial decisions prioritizing marketability over cultural integrity. Collaboration itself becomes marketable, allowing Disney to profit from appearing culturally responsible while maintaining extractive practices.

Destroying Music Cultures

Through musical appropriation, Disney dismantles entire music cultures by severing sounds from the social organizations and cultural contexts that give them meaning. When Disney's production process isolates sonic elements to use as aural shorthands, it destroys the music culture: the social relations of performance, material culture, and ideas about music are all subordinated to Western superculture. The advisory process functions extractively‚Äîconsultants provide cultural data that Western decision-makers recontextualize for commercial consumption, while source communities lose control over how their music cultures are represented globally.

What audiences learn from such films is a Western superculture's version of these music cultures: non-Western musical traditions provide exotic enrichment to Western narratives, require Western framing to achieve emotional legitimacy, and see their own interpretations rendered irrelevant.

Weighing Progress Against Persistent Limitations

Fair interpretation requires acknowledging procedural improvements. Phase II consultants gained unprecedented platform and creative input. Indigenous artists like Foa'i and Fjellheim achieved global visibility, reaching hundreds of millions. Young Polynesian and S√°mi viewers encounter characters who speak their languages‚Äîrepresentation holding psychological value absent from earlier complete erasure. Reception among source communities varies: some celebrate any visibility while others critique persistent power imbalances.

Yet acknowledging advances must not preclude structural critique. The improvements demonstrate evolution within hegemonic systems, not liberation from them. Disney progressed from complete exclusion to limited inclusion, from denying consultation to marketing it. These are meaningful steps that nonetheless preserve Western dominance. Gramsci's hegemonic adaptation explains the shift: rather than rejecting critique about racist representation, Disney absorbed it by inviting consultants into the process while retaining final creative command. The system appears more ethical because of its inclusion of diverse voices, yet the fundamental architecture of decision-making remains unchanged.

Conclusion: Sophisticated Hegemony, Not Decolonization

Disney's evolution in representing non-Western cultures constitutes sophisticated hegemonic adaptation, not genuine decolonization. The corporation reformed methods without relinquishing structural power. The trajectory demonstrates Gramsci's theory: dominant systems absorb critique to maintain power rather than redistribute it. In the 1990s, the mechanism was Orientalist exclusion and crude stereotyping. Today, consultative appropriation invites "Others" into the room without handing over creative authority.

Visible improvements in recent film scores from Moana and Frozen II should not obscure persistent reality: Western composers retain final authority and non-Western elements accessorize Western frameworks. When executives tell Polynesian composers to revise for "English ears," when they substitute Scandinavian kulning for S√°mi joik, when they simplify Colombian bambuco for "broad appeal"‚Äîthese decisions reveal the persistent logic of the culture industry. Economic imperatives position Western musical conventions as "universal" while treating non-Western structures as financial risk.

The Broader Significance

The significance extends beyond Disney. These patterns shape how billions encounter cultural differences during childhood's formative stages, conditioning audiences to perceive Western musical conventions as emotionally sophisticated and universal while non-Western traditions appear as exotic enrichment. For children from represented cultures, the films teach that their musical heritage requires Western framing to achieve validity‚Äîcultural hegemony's ultimate function.

True decolonization requires redistributing creative authority. Until Indigenous and non-Western creators hold power to define their own sonic narratives by structuring films around their musical traditions rather than Western conventions, Disney's music will remain a sophisticated instrument of Western hegemony disguised as respect. The corporation has reformed its methods without relinquishing structural control. The song may sound more inclusive, but the conductors remain unchanged.
