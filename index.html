Disney's Musical Representation of Non-Western Cultures

From Orientalism to Commodified Inclusion: A Critical Timeline (1953-2021)

The Walt Disney Company occupies a globally influential position within the landscape of cultural production. Its animated features, theme parks, and media conglomerates widely propagate its narratives, critically shaping worldviews of audiences. Today, Disney's works are globally consumed cultural artifacts that have been integral to entertainment for generations.

Disney is not only a storyteller, but also a commercial Western entity with market-driven imperatives. Walt Disney believed in the "terrific power of music," asserting that films would be "dragging and boring" without it; music imbues them with "life and vitality" (Tietyen, 13-14). Following this philosophy, Disney expertly leverages music to propel narratives and establish depicted identities. Yet, just as music communicates culture, music also transmits ideas and concepts. Thus, Disney’s influences must be rigorously scrutinized, as the corporation prescribes ideologies beneficial to Western dominance.

In recent film releases, Disney has noticeably incorporated more accurate depictions. However, critics question whether Disney's efforts genuinely constitute cultural appreciation or merely sophisticated forms of appropriation.

The Argument

Although Disney has replaced the stereotyped music of its earlier films with collaborative compositions of its modern releases, this methodological shift masks a continuity in power. Current inclusion strategies only serve to insulate the corporation from critique, allowing it to commodify cultural difference while maintaining strict Western control over the final product.

Central Question: Has Disney's evolution in representing non-Western cultures constituted genuine decolonization or merely sophisticated adaptation of cultural hegemony?

Theoretical Framework

This analysis draws from six critical theoretical frameworks. Each illuminates different dimensions of how Disney's musical choices operate ideologically:

Orientalism

Edward Said

How Western culture constructs non-Western societies as exotic, inferior "Others" through representation

Cultural Hegemony

Antonio Gramsci

Dominant groups maintain power through consent, adapting to absorb critique rather than relinquishing control

Music as Superculture

Titon & Slobin

Power hierarchies between dominant musical systems and subordinated traditions in cultural contexts

Commodity Racism

bell hooks

Diversity serves commercial interests as marketable "spice" while preserving structural inequality

The Culture Industry

Adorno & Horkheimer

Mass media subordinates artistic integrity to profit, standardizing culture for marketability

Cultural Appropriation

Peter Manuel

Dominant groups extract cultural elements without redistributing power or profits to source communities

Methodology

This study categorizes Disney's evolution into distinct periods based on their approach to cultural representation. What emerges is a pattern of adaptation: as each methodology faces critique, Disney evolves its methods while preserving structural control. The corporation invites "Others" into the room—but has not handed over the baton.

Pre-Phase I

1950s

Overt Caricature

Exemplar: Peter Pan (1953) deployed sonic markers to construct Indigenous peoples as primitive spectacle. "What Made the Red Man Red?" exemplified entertainment as explicit racist discourse, with no pretense of authenticity or respect. Complete exclusion of represented communities defined this era.

Phase I

1990s-2000s

Orientalism & Sanitization

Exemplars: Aladdin (1992), Pocahontas (1995), Mulan (1998). The Renaissance Era replaced crude caricature with "aural shorthand"—pentatonic scales for "Asian," augmented seconds for "Middle Eastern." Composers deployed pre-existing colonial tropes as superficial signification. Pocahontas sanitized genocide through pop ballads; Mulan coded Eastern values as oppressive, Western as liberating. Cultural creators remained excluded from decision-making.

Phase II

2010s-2020s

Consultative Appropriation

Exemplars: Moana (2016), Frozen II (2019), Encanto (2021). Responding to criticism, Disney implemented formalized collaboration. Surface-level authenticity improved: Indigenous languages, traditional instruments, visual accuracy. However, consultants provide input while Western executives make final decisions. Managed inclusion extracts legitimacy without redistributing authority.

Discussion: From Orientalism to Consultative Appropriation

The comparative analysis demonstrates that while Disney's methods have evolved from exclusion to consultation, fundamental mechanisms of cultural power remain structurally intact. Phase I exemplified Said's Orientalism, specifically Arab and Indigenous cultures constructed as exotic Others through stereotypical musical signifiers and complete exclusion from creative processes. Phase II evolved rather than eliminated Orientalist logic. Disney now incorporates non-Western creators on terms that preserve Western authority.

Gramsci's hegemonic adaptation explains the shift. Rather than rejecting critique about racist representation, Disney absorbed it by inviting consultants into the process while retaining final creative command. The system appears more ethical because of its inclusion of diverse voices, yet the fundamental architecture of decision-making remains unchanged. Consultation creates moral cover for continued appropriation.

The Economic Imperative: Why Structural Change Remains Constrained

The culture industry framework reveals why structural change remains impossible. Drawing from decades of market data, Disney executives assume that Western musical conventions convey an "universal" emotional language that ensures profitability, while non-Western structures represent financial risk.

Disney's musical choices directly reflect the culture industry's standardization imperative. Systematic "wrapping" in Moana and substituting kulning for joik in Frozen II are calculated commercial decisions prioritizing marketability over cultural integrity. Non-Western musical structures alone cannot drive narratives because doing so would violate the industry's standardization formulae. Instead, the industry jams non-Western elements into Western frameworks to guarantee success.

Recently, the culture industry seems to have commodified the consultative process itself. Displays of collaboration offer marketable appeal, allowing Disney to profit from appearing culturally responsible while maintaining extractive practices. The pattern illustrates the culture industry's capacity to adapt to critique: what appears as ethical progress becomes another product feature enhancing commercial appeal. Moreover, under hooks’ commodity racism, diversity also sells because it offers marketable difference without disrupting the Western foundation.

Destroying Music Cultures

Through musical appropriation, Disney dismantles entire music cultures by severing sounds from the social organizations and cultural contexts that give them meaning. When Disney's production process isolates sonic elements to use as aural shorthands, it destroys the music culture: the social relations of performance, material culture, and ideas about music are all subordinated to Western superculture. The advisory process functions extractively—consultants provide cultural data that Western decision-makers recontextualize for commercial consumption, while source communities lose control over how their music cultures are represented globally.

What audiences learn from such films is a Western superculture's version of these music cultures: non-Western musical traditions provide exotic enrichment to Western narratives, require Western framing to achieve emotional legitimacy, and see their own interpretations rendered irrelevant.

Weighing Progress Against Persistent Limitations

Disney’s procedural improvements are still wins. Phase II consultants gained an unprecedented platform and more creative input. Indigenous artists like Foa'i and Fjellheim achieved global visibility, reaching hundreds of millions. Young Polynesian and Sámi viewers encounter characters who speak their languages and wear their clothes and sing their songs (Arctic Council, 2020).

Reception among source communities also varies. Some celebrate any visibility while others critique persistent power imbalances (Artic Record, 2020). Structural critique doesn't negate the value some community members find in Disney's evolving representations. Yet, the improvements demonstrate evolution within hegemonic systems, not liberation from them. While these are meaningful steps, they nonetheless preserve Western dominance.

Conclusion: Sophisticated Hegemony, Not Decolonization

Disney's evolution in representing non-Western cultures constitutes sophisticated hegemonic adaptation, not genuine decolonization. The corporation reformed methods without relinquishing structural power. The trajectory demonstrates Gramsci's theory: dominant systems absorb critique to maintain power rather than redistribute it. In the 1990s, the mechanism was Orientalist exclusion and crude stereotyping. Today, consultative appropriation invites "Others" into the room without handing over creative authority.

Visible improvements in recent film scores from Moana and Frozen II should not obscure persistent reality: Western composers retain final authority and non-Western elements accessorize Western frameworks.

The Broader Significance

The significance extends beyond Disney. These patterns shape how billions encounter cultural differences during childhood's formative stages, conditioning audiences to perceive Western musical conventions as emotionally sophisticated and universal while non-Western traditions appear as exotic enrichment. For children from represented cultures, the films teach that their musical heritage seems to require Western framing to achieve validity.

True decolonization requires redistributing creative authority. Until Indigenous and non-Western creators hold power to define their own sonic narratives by structuring films around their musical traditions rather than Western conventions, Disney's music will remain a sophisticated instrument of Western hegemony disguised as respect.
