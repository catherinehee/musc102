Moana

2016

Phase II: The Modern Era

Overview: The Shift to "Consultative Appropriation"

Moana (2016) represents Disney's response to decades of criticism about cultural representation. The studio partnered with the Oceanic Cultural Trust and Polynesian composer Opetaia Foa'i, marking a visible shift from outright exclusion to formalized collaboration. However, this case study reveals how collaboration can serve to extract cultural legitimacy while maintaining Western structural control.

The Oceanic Cultural Trust: A New Model?

Disney established the Oceanic Cultural Trust (OCT), bringing together anthropologists, linguists, choreographers, and cultural practitioners from across the Pacific. On the surface, this represents meaningful progress — Indigenous voices were invited into the creative process.

What Changed in Phase II

  • Formalized collaboration with cultural consultants
  • Indigenous musicians involved in composition
  • Use of Pacific languages in the soundtrack
  • Visual accuracy in cultural details
  • Marketing emphasized "authenticity"

However, as scholar Michelle Anya Anjirbag argues, this methodology reveals "embedded coloniality" — where inclusion strategies serve to insulate the corporation from critique while preserving Western decision-making power.

"We Know The Way": Contained Authenticity

Although Polynesian musician Opetaia Foa'i contributed distinct rhythms and lyrics, Western composers Lin-Manuel Miranda and Mark Mancina retained ultimate creative authority. Ethnomusicological analysis uncovers that Polynesian elements are systematically "wrapped" within Western musical tropes. Namely, the songs' starts and ends utilize Western orchestration and harmonic structures to frame its Polynesian sounds.

Even "We Know the Way" (Miranda, Foa'i, and Mancina), featuring Foa'i's compositions, adheres to Western pop verse-chorus structures and is performed by Miranda. This "wrapping" signals to global audiences that Indigenous elements are acceptable only when contained within recognizable American conventions. Polynesian musical contributions provide an additional flair of culture without disrupting the Western musical foundation.

"Polynesian musical contributions provide an additional flair of authenticity without disrupting the Western musical foundation."

— Analysis of Moana's embedded coloniality

"How Far I'll Go": The Universal (Western) Core

The film's emotional centerpiece, "How Far I'll Go," is a standard Broadway power ballad composed by Lin-Manuel Miranda. While Moana is Polynesian, her "I want" song — the moment of deepest emotional revelation — is musically indistinguishable from any contemporary Disney princess song.

The Pattern Continues

  • Cultural specificity = atmosphere and setting
  • Western conventions = emotional depth and character development
  • The "universal" is defined by Western musical structures
  • Non-Western music positioned as "ethnic flavor," not narrative core

This structural choice communicates ideology: authentic Polynesian music is welcome when it provides cultural texture, but Western musical language remains necessary for "universal" emotional resonance.

"Revise It for English Ears": The Limits of Collaboration

In an interview, Foa'i notes how Disney instructed him to revise his compositions for "English ears," which further highlights how marketability was prioritized over authenticity.

— Madeleine Chapman, "Moana Composer Opetaia Foa'i on Keeping Disney's Moana Authentic through Music" (2017)

This single detail reveals everything about the power dynamics of Phase II collaboration. Foa'i was invited to the table — but Disney instructed (not asked) him to modify his work for commercial viability. "English ears" becomes code for Western market expectations overriding cultural authenticity.

As Rebecca Armstrong argues, the advisory process extracts legitimacy rather than relinquishing actual creative authority. It exemplifies commodity racism: the corporation adopts a collaborative image only to placate its audiences, while preserving the decision-making power. Disney gains protection from criticism ("We worked with Polynesian consultants!") while maintaining final control over the product.

Commodity Racism in Action

bell hooks describes "commodity racism" as a process where ethnicity functions as "spice, seasoning that can liven up the dull dish that is mainstream white culture." Moana exemplifies this dynamic perfectly:

How Moana Commodifies Polynesian Culture

  • Polynesian elements provide "authentic" flavor and marketability
  • Western structures organize and commercialize that authenticity
  • Indigenous collaborators give legitimacy to Western-controlled product
  • Profits flow to Western corporation, not Pacific communities
  • Cultural specificity subordinated to "universal" (Western) appeal

The film appeals to Disney's increasingly diverse audiences and placates past critiques, while preserving the corporation's decision-making power. This is hegemony disguised as diversity.

The Economic Imperative

Why doesn't Disney simply allow Polynesian composers full creative authority? The answer is economic: executives operate under the assumption that Western musical conventions constitute a "universal" emotional language, while non-Western structures represent financial risk.

This assumption is never tested — instead, it functions as self-fulfilling prophecy. Audiences are conditioned to accept Western structures as neutral and universal through repeated exposure, while non-Western music remains coded as "ethnic" and "specific."

What This Teaches Audiences

Moana functions as a pedagogical tool, teaching global audiences — including Polynesian children — that while cultures may differ visually and linguistically, "real" emotion must be expressed through Western musical forms. This promotes cultural homogenization where non-Western sounds serve as atmospheric decoration for Western narratives.

"The films function as pedagogical tools that train audiences—including those from the represented cultures—to perceive Western aesthetics as 'natural' and their own traditional forms as 'exotic.'"

— Analysis of Disney's cultural hegemony

Progress or Adaptation?

Moana represents undeniable improvements over Aladdin and Pocahontas:

  • Genuine cultural consultation
  • Indigenous language inclusion
  • Polynesian musical contributions
  • Visual and cultural accuracy
  • Positive representation benefits

However, these improvements occur within an unchanged power structure. The corporation has adapted its methods to the era of identity politics by inviting "Others" into the room — but it has not handed over the baton. Western composers retain final authority, non-Western elements serve Western frameworks, and profits remain centralized.

This is not decolonization. It is sophisticated colonialism.
