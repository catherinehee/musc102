Peter Pan

1953

Pre-Phase I: Foundational Colonial Mechanism

Overview: The Foundation of Disney's "Othering"

The history of Disney's non-Western musical representation is rooted in earlier, overt displays of caricature, exemplified by the 1953 film Peter Pan (Lugo-Lugo & King, 2010). This film employed a foundational colonial mechanism that predates Phase I, relying on crude, generalized sonic markers and amalgamation to signal exoticism. The song "What Made the Red Man Red?" explicitly reinforced racist discourse, utilizing music to perpetuate stereotypes under the guise of entertainment.

"What Made the Red Man Red?": Racist Caricature as Entertainment

The song's composition and context reveal its primary function as an exercise in "othering." It demonstrates explicit racist discourse presented as children's entertainment.

Musical Stereotyping and Exoticism

The score features:

  • Chanting presented as "primitive" vocalization
  • Pervasive use of drums to signal "Indianness"
  • Invented gibberish like the refrain "Hana Mana Ganda"
  • Simplistic rhythmic patterns that became stereotypes in subsequent media

This generalized portrayal was intended to sound 'Indian' to a Western audience (Lugo-Lugo & King 2010). The use of simplistic drum patterns and repetitive melodies became a stereotype affixed to Native Americans in subsequent media (Anderson 2019).

The music isn't attempting to reference any actual Indigenous musical traditions. Instead, it constructs what Western audiences expect "Indian" music to sound like based on existing Hollywood stereotypes.

Reinforcement of Colonial Hierarchy

The song attempts to answer the questions posed by the white Lost Boys regarding the origins of Indigenous people, including why they are "red" and why they say "How" and "Ugh". The lyrics include derogatory terms like "Injuns" and "squaw". The explanation provided sexualizes indigeneity itself; lyrics reason that Indigenous people became "red" when an ancestor blushed after a kiss, suggesting "sexual anticipation" (Wainer, n.d.).

The entire musical sequence is structurally designed to frame Native Americans in a stereotypical manner, presenting them as inferior to the white protagonists and normalizing negative stereotypes.

The Precedent It Established

This musical approach established a precedent for reducing non-Western cultures to generalized, easily consumable tropes. Even when drawing from source material, the Disney adaptation "doubled-down on racial stereotypes," cementing the practice of prioritizing Western caricature over respectful cultural specificity (Laskow n.d.).

The techniques pioneered here—invented "ethnic" sounds, pervasive drums, simplistic rhythms, framing "Others" through white gaze—would continue (in modified form) through Phase I films like Aladdin and Pocahontas. This early film demonstrates how the music functions ideologically to normalize racial prejudice and shape perceptions of minority groups as "other".
