Frozen II

2019

Phase II: The Modern Era

Overview: Negotiating Power Differentials

Following criticism of the original Frozen (2013) for its superficial use of generalized Scandinavian motifs, Disney implemented a formalized collaborative model for Frozen II (2019). The corporation partnered with verddet, an advisory group comprising Sámi artists, elders, and cultural experts. This case reveals the limits of collaboration when creative authority remains non-negotiable.

The Criticism of Frozen (2013)

The original Frozen utilized a generic "frozen north" aesthetic without meaningful engagement with Sámi culture, despite the story's setting in a Nordic-inspired kingdom. While the film featured Frode Fjellheim's joik-inspired "Vuelie" in the opening, this was largely decorative, and Sámi communities criticized the lack of genuine representation.

Problems with Frozen (2013)

  • Generic Nordic fantasy aesthetic
  • No meaningful Sámi consultation
  • Joik used decoratively without cultural context
  • Sámi-inspired Kristoff as minor, comedic character

This criticism prompted Disney to adopt a more collaborative approach for the sequel — but the question remains whether this collaboration represented genuine power-sharing or managed inclusion.

Verddet: The Advisory Model

For Frozen II, Disney partnered with verddet, which included Sámi artists, reindeer herders, historians, and cultural practitioners. This collaboration successfully improved visual accuracy — Sámi clothing designs (gákti), reindeer practices, and cultural details were respectfully rendered.

However, the musical soundscape remained firmly under Western authority. While visual consultants had meaningful input, sonic decisions were subject to negotiation, modification, and ultimately, erasure.

Kulning vs. Joik: A Revealing Substitution

The film's central mystery revolves around a mystical voice calling to Elsa. This voice uses kulning — a Scandinavian cattle-calling technique — rather than joik, the traditional Sámi form of vocalization.

Why This Matters

  • Joik is culturally specific to the Sámi people
  • Kulning is a broader Nordic/Scandinavian practice
  • The story explicitly engages with Sámi-inspired Northuldra people
  • Kulning aligns with Western expectations of "magical" Nordic fantasy
  • Substitution reflects commercial over ethnomusicological priorities

Kulning was chosen because it fit Western audience expectations of what "Nordic magic" sounds like. It's ethereal, haunting, and familiar from other fantasy media. Joik, meanwhile, has specific cultural and spiritual meanings that don't conform to generic fantasy aesthetics.

"Hey Na Na Music": Eurocentric Dismissal

Composer Christophe Beck dismissed joik as "Hey Na Na music," reflecting a Eurocentric refusal to engage with the complexity of Indigenous musical traditions.

— Mihaela Mihailova, "Negotiable Diversity" (2025)

This quote reveals everything about Phase II's limitations. Beck's dismissive characterization reduces a sophisticated Indigenous vocal tradition to nonsense syllables. This is not ignorance — it's a refusal to engage with musical complexity that doesn't conform to Western expectations.

Joik is not simple "Hey Na Na" vocalizations. It's a deeply sophisticated practice where each joik is uniquely composed for a specific person, place, or animal, evoking essence rather than describing. It operates on fundamentally different principles than Western melody-based composition.

Beck's dismissal demonstrates that even in Phase II's "collaborative" model, Indigenous musical traditions can be rejected if they don't fit established Disney aesthetics.

"Vuelie": Decontextualized Authenticity

When authentic Sámi music does appear — such as Frode Fjellheim's joik-based composition "Vuelie" in the opening sequence — it is decontextualized from its spiritual and cultural origins, abstracted into a generic fantasy atmosphere.

"Sámi culture enriches the film's texture but does not define its sonic identity. Its elements are only included as long as it does not disrupt the established Disney aesthetic and hinder maximum commercial appeal."

— Analysis of Frozen II's cultural negotiation

The presence of Sámi musical elements serves to provide authenticity and protect Disney from criticism, but these elements function as seasoning, not substance. The core musical language remains firmly Western.

Visual Success, Sonic Subordination

The contrast between visual and sonic representation is instructive. Verddet consultants had meaningful input on visual elements, resulting in respectful representation of Sámi material culture. However, music — the medium that communicates emotion and drives narrative — remained non-negotiable.

The Pattern of Selective Collaboration

  • Visual: Accurate gákti designs, reindeer practices, living patterns
  • Linguistic: Consultation on cultural concepts and terminology
  • Musical: Western control maintained; Indigenous forms rejected or decorative

This hierarchy reveals what Disney considers negotiable versus essential. Visual accuracy can be achieved without threatening commercial viability, but sonic identity — the emotional core — must remain Western to ensure market success.

The Broadway Default

Like Moana, Frozen II's emotional centerpiece songs — "Into the Unknown," "Show Yourself" — are standard Broadway power ballads composed by Kristen Anderson-Lopez and Robert Lopez. While Elsa's journey involves Sámi-inspired Indigenous people and their cultural knowledge, her emotional expression remains musically indistinguishable from any Western musical theater production.

The message is clear: Sámi culture can provide visual atmosphere and narrative conflict, but Western musical language structures "universal" emotional truth.

Improved Yet Unchanged

Frozen II represents meaningful progress in many ways:

  • Formalized collaboration with Indigenous advisory board
  • Respectful visual representation of Sámi culture
  • Engagement with colonial history (dam narrative)
  • Positive representation for Sámi communities

However, these improvements coexist with persistent structural control. The collaboration model allows Disney to market authenticity while retaining final authority over creative decisions. When Indigenous musical traditions conflict with commercial imperatives or aesthetic expectations, they are rejected, modified, or relegated to decorative roles.

This is not partnership — it is managed consultation designed to extract legitimacy while preserving power.

What Genuine Collaboration Would Look Like

True decolonization of cultural representation would require:

  • Sámi composers with final authority over musical decisions
  • Centering Indigenous musical structures for emotional core, not just atmosphere
  • Rejecting the assumption that Western conventions equal "universal" appeal
  • Profit-sharing with represented communities
  • Creative control, not just consultative input

Until these conditions are met, even the most well-intentioned collaboration serves to modernize cultural hegemony rather than dismantle it.
