Frozen II

2019

Phase II: The Modern Era

Overview: Negotiating Power Differentials

Following criticism of the original Frozen (2013) for its superficial use of generalized Scandinavian motifs, Disney implemented a formalized collaborative model for Frozen II (2019). The corporation partnered with verddet, an advisory group comprising Sámi artists, elders, and cultural experts. This case reveals the limits of collaboration when creative authority remains non-negotiable.

The Criticism of Frozen (2013)

The original Frozen utilized a generic "frozen north" aesthetic without meaningful engagement with Sámi culture, despite the story's setting in a Nordic-inspired kingdom. While the film featured Frode Fjellheim's joik-inspired "Vuelie" in the opening, this was largely decorative, and Sámi communities criticized the lack of genuine representation.

Problems with Frozen (2013)

  • Generic Nordic fantasy aesthetic
  • No meaningful Sámi consultation
  • Joik used decoratively without cultural context
  • Sámi-inspired Kristoff as minor, comedic character

This criticism prompted Disney to adopt a more collaborative approach for the sequel — but the question remains whether this collaboration represented genuine power-sharing or managed inclusion.

Verddet: The Advisory Model

For Frozen II, Disney partnered with verddet, which included Sámi artists, reindeer herders, historians, and cultural practitioners. This collaboration successfully improved visual accuracy — Sámi clothing designs (gákti), reindeer practices, and cultural details were respectfully rendered.

However, the musical soundscape remained firmly under Western authority. While visual consultants had meaningful input, sonic decisions were subject to negotiation, modification, and ultimately, erasure.

Kulning vs. Joik: A Revealing Substitution

The film also substitutes kulning (non-Sámi Scandinavian cattle call) for joik in the mysterious voice calling Elsa, despite their distinct ethnic roots. Kulning aligns with Western expectations of "magical" Nordic fantasy; joik might sound too unfamiliar.

Kulning was chosen because it fit Western audience expectations of what "Nordic magic" sounds like — ethereal, haunting, and familiar from other fantasy media. Joik, meanwhile, has specific cultural and spiritual meanings that don't conform to generic fantasy aesthetics. Thus, although Sámi traditions enrich the film's texture, they do not define its sonic identity. Disney only includes elements when they do not disrupt the established Disney aesthetic.

"Vuelie": From Exotic Atmosphere to Decontextualized Ritual

Conversely, the musical soundscape, under the direction of Western composer Christophe Beck, remains subject to erasure of Indigenous specificity. "Vuelie," the franchise's iconic opening chant composed by Sámi musician Frode Fjellheim, mixes the "chant-like" joik, the traditional Sámi vocal form, with classical vocal style co-written with Beck. Originally, Frozen (2013) featured "Vuelie" without acknowledging its origins, using it solely to score the film's exotic atmosphere. The composition was used for its "memorably melodic opening tune," not its cultural significance as a Sámi chant.

Frozen II (2019) attempted to rectify this by featuring the song in a ritual welcoming Elsa and Anna into the Northuldra community, mimicking the Sámi tradition of joiking for acceptance into society. Yet the performance still relied on the non-Sámi Cantus choir rather than Sámi voices, perpetuating what scholars term "slippage between 'Sámi' and 'Scandinavian'" and recontextualizing the ritual into background music. The scene provides no explanation of the ritual's cultural meaning, trivializing it into background music instead.

Lead composer Beck also mischaracterizes "Vuelie" as "Hey Na Na music" and refers to joiking as a "Northern European yodel" and "Scandinavian element," obscuring its Indigenous Sámi roots in favor of vague geographic markers that erase cultural specificity. This failure by a key creative figure to correctly identify cultural tradition directly undermines Disney's public claims of respectful cultural stewardship.
