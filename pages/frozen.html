Frozen

2013

Transition Period

Overview: The Critique That Changed Everything

Frozen (2013) became a cultural phenomenon and Disney's highest-grossing animated film at the time. However, its treatment of Scandinavian and particularly Sámi cultural elements drew criticism that would fundamentally change Disney's approach to cultural representation. This film represents a transition point between Phase I's crude stereotyping and Phase II's consultative model.

"Let It Go": Global Phenomenon

"Let It Go" became a global phenomenon, topping charts in multiple countries and winning the Academy Award for Best Original Song. Composed by Kristen Anderson-Lopez and Robert Lopez, it's a quintessential Broadway power ballad — musically, it could be set anywhere, at any time.

The song exemplifies what had become Disney's standard approach: use Western musical theater conventions for emotional core while cultural specificity remains visual. The Norwegian/Nordic setting functions as aesthetic backdrop, not sonic identity.

The Generic "Frozen North"

Frozen presented a generic Nordic fantasy aesthetic — fjords, reindeer, ice palaces, and vague Scandinavian cultural markers. While the film featured some Norwegian-inspired visual elements, it made no meaningful engagement with Sámi culture, despite the region's Indigenous people.

Cultural Elements in Frozen (2013)

  • Kristoff's character loosely inspired by Sámi ice harvesters
  • Reindeer herding as comedic backdrop
  • Opening features Frode Fjellheim's joik-inspired "Vuelie"
  • No Sámi consultation or meaningful representation
  • Sámi elements used decoratively for "exotic" flavor

"Vuelie": Decorative Authenticity

The film's opening features "Vuelie" (also known as "Eatnemen Vuelie" or "Song of the Earth"), composed by Norwegian Sámi musician Frode Fjellheim. This piece authentically incorporates joik — the traditional Sámi form of song.

However, this authentic element functions as exotic opening flavor, quickly giving way to standard Western orchestral and Broadway musical conventions for the film's narrative core. Joik appears and disappears, serving as cultural seasoning rather than structural foundation.

The use of joik in the opening creates an "exotic" atmosphere that signals "Northern magic" to audiences, but the music is decontextualized from its cultural and spiritual meanings.

— Analysis of Frozen's cultural borrowing

The Sámi Criticism

Sámi communities and cultural advocates criticized Frozen for appropriating visual and cultural elements without consultation, representation, or acknowledgment. Key concerns included:

Sámi Community Concerns

  • Kristoff portrayed as comedic, uncultured side character
  • No acknowledgment of Sámi people in film or credits
  • Sámi-inspired elements used without consultation
  • Generic Nordic fantasy erased specific Indigenous identity
  • Profited from cultural borrowing without community benefit

This criticism reflected broader concerns about cultural appropriation in media — taking cultural elements for commercial gain while excluding the communities those elements come from. For Sámi people, who have faced centuries of colonization and cultural suppression, this felt like a continuation of historical patterns.

The Turning Point

The criticism of Frozen came at a cultural moment when audiences were increasingly attuned to issues of representation and cultural appropriation. Social media amplified these concerns, and Disney faced pressure to respond.

This pressure led to a significant shift in corporate strategy. For Frozen II, Disney would implement a formalized collaborative model, partnering with verddet, a Sámi advisory group. This marked the corporation's explicit acknowledgment that its previous approach was insufficient.

What Frozen Reveals About Phase I Logic

Despite being released in 2013, Frozen operated largely on Phase I logic:

  • Visual borrowing without consultation: Taking cultural elements as aesthetic resources
  • Decorative authenticity: Using joik for exotic flavor, not narrative structure
  • Western musical core: Broadway conventions for all emotional depth
  • Erasure: No acknowledgment of Sámi people despite cultural borrowing

However, unlike Aladdin and Pocahontas, Frozen faced immediate, organized criticism that Disney could not ignore. The cultural conversation had shifted, and the corporation would need to adapt.

The Lessons Learned (Sort Of)

Frozen's success — over $1.28 billion at the box office — proved that cultural specificity was not necessary for commercial success. Yet the criticism revealed that audiences and advocacy groups were increasingly unwilling to accept uncritical cultural appropriation.

Disney's response with Frozen II would attempt to thread this needle: maintain commercial appeal through familiar Western musical structures while adding visible markers of consultation and respect. Whether this represents genuine progress or sophisticated adaptation of power remains the central question of Phase II analysis.

Legacy and Impact

Frozen remains one of Disney's most successful and beloved films. "Let It Go" became an anthem of self-acceptance for millions. The film's positive impacts — particularly for young audiences exploring identity and familial love — are real and meaningful.

However, its treatment of Sámi culture demonstrates how even well-intentioned creative work can perpetuate patterns of cultural erasure when power remains concentrated in Western institutions. The film's legacy includes both its cultural impact and the important conversations it sparked about representation, consultation, and cultural responsibility.
