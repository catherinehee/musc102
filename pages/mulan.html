Mulan

1998

Phase I: The Renaissance Era

Overview: Musical Orientalism and Ideological Binaries

The music of Mulan (1998) exemplifies musical Orientalism, where Disney abstracted Chinese cultural elements and reconfigured them to conform to Western commercial demands. Although the film's plot is based on Chinese folklore, the score prioritized Euro-American musical structures while using stereotypical "Asian-sounding" elements to create exotic atmosphere.

The Orientalist Musical Architecture

The score, composed by non-Chinese American Jerry Goldsmith, prioritized Western symphonic structures deemed necessary to remain "palatable" to Western audiences. This decision reveals a crucial assumption: Chinese musical traditions are insufficient for conveying universal emotion to global (read: Western) audiences.

"Honor to Us All": Pentatonic Shorthand

"Honor to Us All" relies on broad musical stereotypes, specifically pentatonic scales and generalized melodic phrases that American viewers had long been conditioned to interpret as "Asian-sounding." This is the 1990s version of Peter Pan's drums and chanting—more sophisticated, but operating on the same logic.

What Pentatonic Scales Signal

  • Used across many Asian musical traditions, but not uniquely Chinese
  • Functions as generalized "Orient" marker in Western media
  • Creates instant recognition for Western audiences: "This is Asia"
  • Actual Chinese musical complexity reduced to recognizable pattern

Like the augmented second in Aladdin, the pentatonic scale functions as "aural shorthand"— a sonic stereotype that signals exoticism without requiring genuine engagement with Chinese musical traditions. It's what Western audiences expect Chinese music to sound like, based on decades of Hollywood Orientalism.

Musical Binaries: East vs. West as Repression vs. Liberation

Mulan's score reinforces an ideological binary: Eastern values are musically coded as repressive and backwards, while Western values are coded as liberating and progressive. This isn't subtle—it's a systematic pattern throughout the film.
