Encanto

2021

Phase II: The Modern Era

A Successful Collaboration?

The musical representation in Encanto (2021) is one of Disney's more successful examples of its new collaborative paradigm, succeeding in cultural celebration by foregrounding inclusion.

Encanto successfully showcases and celebrates Colombian heritage, distancing the country from previous negative stereotypes of drug trafficking and violence (Jiménez, 2024). Its soundtrack—developed after Lin-Manuel Miranda and the filmmaking team conducted research on the ground in Colombia—integrates distinctly local musical elements, including traditional genres such as bambuco and vallenato, and instruments like the marimba and arpa llanera. Germaine Franco, an American-born Latina composer, infused the score with Colombian rhythmic structures and collaborated with Colombian recording artists, most notably Sebastián Yatra.

This commitment to structural inclusion, where culturally affiliated creators hold key roles, helped deliver a nuanced, affirming narrative that highlights the Colombian values of family and tradition. The soundtrack received widespread critical acclaim and monumental commercial success, demonstrating that films with authentic cultural foundations can achieve massive global popularity.

Notably, "Dos Oruguitas" is an all Spanish-language song that critics perceive to be Encanto's "heart and soul" (Billboard, 2022). Interestingly, even in its separately released English adaptation, the song is still titled "Two Oruguitas," preserving the original Spanish title (Oruguitas translates to Little Caterpillars).

Cultural Amalgamation

Despite high praise, even Encanto demonstrates a persistent trend toward cultural amalgamation and centralized control. While the music draws on Colombian specifics, the soundtrack blends distinct ethnocultural sounds into broader, marketable pan-Latin genres (Jiménez, 2024).

"The Dysfunctional Tango"

The film also features a track titled "The Dysfunctional Tango" in an Argentine style (Jiménez, 2024).

"Waiting on a Miracle"

The film's attempt to incorporate the Colombian bambuco genre in the song "Waiting on a Miracle" was critiqued for lacking its characteristic polymetric nature where "binary and ternary durations overlap rhythmically [and] binary and ternary melodic articulations alternate" (Jiménez, 2024).

The resulting composition was noted for sounding like generic "folk" rather than authentic bambuco, and its simplified rhythmic character meant the musical arrangement prioritized a Western waltz-like form for broad audience appeal.
