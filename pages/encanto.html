Encanto

2021

Phase II: The Modern Era

Overview: Disney's Most Successful Collaboration

The musical representation in Encanto (2021) is one of Disney's more successful examples of its collaborative paradigm. The film successfully showcases and celebrates Colombian heritage, distancing the country from previous negative stereotypes of drug trafficking and violence. However, even this acclaimed project demonstrates persistent patterns toward cultural amalgamation and centralized control.

What Encanto Does Right

Genuine Colombian Integration

Encanto's soundtrack—developed after Lin-Manuel Miranda and the filmmaking team conducted research on the ground in Colombia—integrates distinctly local musical elements:

  • Traditional genres: Bambuco, vallenato, cumbia
  • Instruments: Marimba, arpa llanera, tiple, guacharaca
  • Colombian rhythmic structures throughout the score
  • Colombian recording artists: Most notably Sebastián Yatra

Structural Inclusion

Germaine Franco, an American-born Latina composer, served as the film's composer—representing meaningful diversity in a role typically reserved for white male composers. This commitment to structural inclusion, where culturally affiliated creators hold key roles, helped deliver a nuanced, affirming narrative that highlights Colombian values of family and tradition.

"Dos Oruguitas": The All-Spanish Triumph

"Dos Oruguitas" is an all-Spanish language song that critics and audiences perceived to be Encanto's best song. The song's success demonstrates that films with authentic cultural foundations can achieve massive global popularity—and that Spanish-language music can be the emotional centerpiece of a Disney film.

Why This Matters

  • Challenges assumption that English = universal emotional language
  • Spanish positioned as emotionally profound, not just "ethnic flavor"
  • Nominated for Academy Award for Best Original Song
  • Proves non-English music can drive mainstream commercial success

This represents genuine progress compared to Phase I films, where emotional core always required English and Western musical conventions.

"We Don't Talk About Bruno": Massive Success with a Problem

"We Don't Talk About Bruno" became a cultural phenomenon, topping the Billboard Hot 100. However, the song primarily uses the guajira rhythm—characteristic of Cuban son, not Colombian music.

Listen to the Comparison

Traditional Cuban Guajira rhythm

While guajira is related to broader Latin American musical traditions, its use in a specifically Colombian story reveals a pattern of cultural amalgamation—blending distinct ethnocultural sounds into broader, marketable pan-Latin genres.

"This deliberate amalgamation contributes to assimilation, where distinct ethnocultural groups are blended into one, implying absorption into the mainstream Latin identity acceptable to a mass audience."

— Jiménez (2024), analysis of Encanto's soundtrack

"The Dysfunctional Tango": Pan-Latin Amalgamation

The film also features "The Dysfunctional Tango" in an Argentine style:

Traditional Argentine Tango for comparison

While this creates musical variety and showcases Latin American diversity, it also raises questions: Why does a Colombian story need Argentine and Cuban musical forms? This suggests a pattern where distinct cultures are homogenized into "Latin" as a marketable category—similar to how Aladdin created a generic "Middle East."

"Waiting on a Miracle": Simplified Bambuco

The film's attempt to incorporate the Colombian bambuco genre in "Waiting on a Miracle" was critiqued for lacking its characteristic polymetric nature, where "binary and ternary durations overlap rhythmically [and] binary and ternary melodic articulations alternate."

Compare to Authentic Bambuco

"The resulting composition was noted for sounding like generic 'folk' rather than authentic bambuco, and its simplified rhythmic character meant the musical arrangement prioritized a Western waltz-like form for broad audience appeal."

— Jiménez (2024)

This reveals the persistent pattern: when authentic cultural forms conflict with broad marketability, they're simplified. The culture industry's logic—standardization for maximum profit—still operates, even within collaborative frameworks.

Commercial Success Proves... What?

Encanto received widespread critical acclaim and monumental commercial success:

  • Soundtrack topped Billboard 200 for multiple weeks
  • "We Don't Talk About Bruno" reached #1 on Hot 100
  • Multiple Academy Award nominations
  • Cultural phenomenon status globally

This success demonstrates that films with authentic cultural foundations can achieve massive global popularity. It challenges executives' assumptions that Western conventions equal commercial viability while non-Western specificity equals financial risk.

However, the success doesn't resolve the underlying question: Would Encanto have been greenlit if Colombian consultants had insisted on polymetric bambuco complexity? If the emotional centerpiece had to be in Spanish from the start? The fact that "Dos Oruguitas" succeeded doesn't answer whether it would have been approved if it hadn't also delivered English songs structured for broad appeal.

Progress Coexisting with Persistent Patterns

Genuine Improvements

  • Latina composer in key creative role
  • Spanish-language song as emotional centerpiece
  • Colombian artists featured and credited
  • Positive, affirming cultural representation
  • Massive commercial success proving viability

Persistent Problems

  • Cultural amalgamation (Cuban, Argentine, Colombian blended)
  • Simplification of complex forms (bambuco → generic "folk")
  • Western waltz structures still organize "ethnic" elements
  • Final creative authority still Western-controlled

Encanto demonstrates that Phase II can produce meaningful representation that benefits depicted communities. The film's positive impact for Colombian diaspora children, who finally see themselves centered in a major animated feature, is real and valuable.

However, even this best-case scenario reveals limits to collaboration without redistribution of creative authority. When bambuco becomes "too complex," it's simplified. When the soundtrack needs variety, other Latin American cultures are blended in. The improvements are real, but they occur within unchanged power structures.

What Encanto Teaches Us About Phase II

If even Disney's most successful cultural collaboration still demonstrates amalgamation and simplification, what does this reveal about the limits of consultative models?

The Pattern Across Phase II

  • Moana: "Revise for English ears"
  • Frozen II: Kulning instead of joik
  • Encanto: Simplified bambuco, pan-Latin amalgamation

In each case, consultants provide authentic elements, but Western producers determine how those elements fit into predetermined structures. Authenticity is welcomed when it enhances marketability, modified when it challenges commercial formulas, and blended when specificity seems too narrow.

Encanto proves that Phase II can produce beautiful, commercially successful, culturally affirming work. But it also reveals that even the best outcomes within this system still operate according to commodity racism's logic: diversity as marketable spice, never replacing the mainstream foundation.
