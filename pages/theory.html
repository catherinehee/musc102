Theoretical Framework

Critical Lenses for Analysis

Introduction

This study employs multiple theoretical frameworks to understand how Disney's musical choices operate ideologically. Each lens reveals different dimensions of power, from the construction of the "Other" to the economic mechanisms that drive cultural extraction.

Music as Music Culture

Key Scholars: Jeff Todd Titon & Mark Slobin

Music cultures encompass not only sonic elements but also ideas about music, social organization, and the contexts that give music meaning. Mark Slobin extends this framework to examine power hierarchies between "supercultures" (dominant musical systems) and "subcultures" (subordinated musical traditions) (Titon, 1996).

Application to Disney

  • Disney extracts musical elements from their original music cultures
  • Recontextualizes them within Western superculture frameworks
  • Teaches audiences to hear non-Western music only through Western paradigms
  • Treats non-Western traditions as repositories of sounds, not living cultures

Orientalism

Key Scholar: Edward Said

Orientalism describes how Western culture constructs non-Western societies as exotic, inferior "Others," justifying dominance through representation. Said's framework reveals that the "Orient" is not a real place but a Western construction—a projection of European fantasies and anxieties (Said, 1978).

Orientalism operates through selective imagery that reduces complex cultures to stereotypes serving Western fantasies (Said, 1978).

— Definition of Orientalism, summarized from Edward Said

Musical Orientalism in Disney

  • Aladdin: Augmented second interval as generic "exotic" marker
  • Mulan: Pentatonic scales signal "Asian-ness"
  • Peter Pan: Drums and chanting for "Indian" stereotypes

Cultural Hegemony

Key Scholar: Antonio Gramsci

Gramsci theorized that dominant groups maintain power through consent rather than force, making their worldview appear as natural truth. Crucially, hegemonic systems adapt to absorb critique rather than relinquish control—they evolve their methods while preserving structural dominance (Gramsci, 1971; El Aidi & Yechouti, 2017).

This explains why Disney can appear to change dramatically while fundamental power dynamics persist. Phase II's "consultative appropriation" is hegemony adapting to contemporary critique.

Cultural Appropriation vs. Appreciation

This study distinguishes appropriation from appreciation according to power dynamics (Manuel, 2021):

Cultural Appreciation

  • Reciprocal exchange between groups of relatively equal power
  • Creative authority fairly distributed
  • Source communities benefit materially and retain control
  • Cultural context preserved and respected

Cultural Appropriation

  • Dominant groups extract cultural elements
  • Power not redistributed to source communities
  • Source communities excluded from creative decisions
  • Profits flow to extractors, not originators

By this definition, even Disney's Phase II collaborations constitute appropriation: Indigenous and non-Western consultants provide input, but Western executives and composers retain final authority over creative decisions and profit distribution.

Commodity Racism

Key Scholar: bell hooks

Ethnicity functions as "spice, seasoning that can liven up the dull dish that is mainstream white culture."

— bell hooks, "Eating the Other" (1992)

Under commodity racism, diversity serves commercial interests while preserving structural inequality. Cultural collaboration becomes marketable, allowing corporations to profit from appearing culturally-conscious without redistributing power. Difference enhances flavor but never replaces the mainstream foundation (hooks, 1992).

Commodity Racism in Disney Films

  • Moana: Polynesian elements provide "authentic" flavor, Western structures organize
  • Encanto: Colombian specificity blended into pan-Latin marketability
  • Frozen II: Sámi culture as exotic texture, not sonic identity
  • Financial motives incentivize extraction over genuine exchange

Schizophonic Mimesis

Key Scholar: Steven Feld

Building on R. Murray Schafer's concept of "schizophonia" (the split between sound and its source through technological reproduction), ethnomusicologist Steven Feld developed "schizophonic mimesis" to describe how sound recordings extract Indigenous sounds and "stimulate and license renegotiations of identity," allowing corporations to textualize and reinvent how global audiences understand Indigenous peoples (Feld, 1996).

Through schizophonic mimesis, sound recordings "split from their sources through technological production and circulation, not only extract Indigenous sounds but stimulate and license renegotiations of identity," enabling corporations to control representations of Indigenous cultures.

— Steven Feld (1996)

Schizophonic Mimesis in Disney

  • Aladdin: Augmented second interval generalized as generic Arab music
  • Moana: Polynesian chants extracted from their genealogical and spiritual functions
  • Frozen II: Sámi joik divorced from its role in reindeer herding and spiritual practice

Connection to Commodity Racism: Commodity racism names the economic motive driving appropriation, while schizophonic mimesis reveals the sonic mechanism through which it operates. Together, these concepts show both the why (commercial profit from difference) and the how (sonic extraction, technological reproduction, and identity renegotiation) of musical appropriation.

The Culture Industry

Key Scholars: Theodor Adorno

Complementing the concept of commodity racism, Adorno coined the term “culture industry” to describe how mass media subordinates artistic integrity to profit maximization. In other words, cultural creations are standardized to ensure marketability. This concept positions Western conventions as "universal" emotional language while treating non-Western structures as financial risk.

Disney operates within capitalist logic that demands formulaic creative approaches for maximum profit. The culture industry reveals why commodity racism persists: profit imperatives drive corporations to appease critiques while preserving extractive practices (Adorno & Rabinbach, 1975).

Synthesis: How These Frameworks Work Together

These theoretical lenses are not separate—they illuminate different dimensions of the same system:

  1. Orientalism constructs the "Other" as inferior and exotic
  2. Cultural hegemony makes this construction feel natural and inevitable to its audiences
  3. Music as superculture/subculture shows how sonic hierarchies operate
  4. Commodity racism transforms difference into marketable product
  5. Schizophonic mimesis reveals the technical process of sonic extraction and recontextualization
  6. The culture industry provides the economic imperative driving the system
