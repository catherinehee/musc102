Aladdin

1992

Phase I: The Renaissance Era

Overview: Orientalism in Action

Aladdin (1992) deploys Orientalist musical tactics that define the Arab world as savage and inferior.

"Arabian Nights": The White Gaze

"Where they cut off your ear / If they don't like your face / It's barbaric, but hey, it's home."

— Original lyrics to 'Arabian Nights' (Menken & Ashman, 1992; later revised after criticism)"

The opening song, "Arabian Nights," initially described the Middle Eastern setting as a place of barbarism and violence. While protests forced a lyrical revision, the retained line, "It's barbaric, but hey, it's home," signaled the film's ideological stance (King et al., 2010). The lyrics frame the setting through a "white gaze," geographically and morally positioning American culture as superior to the depicted "Other."

Musical Orientalism: "Aural Shorthand"

Musically, composers Alan Menken and Howard Ashman utilized "aural shorthands" — recognizable markers of exoticism rather than authentic Arabic music. For example, the augmented second interval is not specific to Arab culture but is a generalized Western signifier for foreignness (Taxidou, 2022). Such aural shorthands expose a crucial power dynamic where European fantasies, not Arab people, define Middle Eastern identity in the film. More fundamentally, Disney treats Arab music culture as a repository of exotic sounds rather than a living tradition with its own significance.

Furthermore, by excluding Arab musicians from the creative process, Disney failed to engage with actual Arab musical traditions, relying instead on Western stereotypes of what 'Oriental' music should sound like.

"A Whole New World": Western Musical Hegemony

"A Whole New World" (Menken & Rice, 1992), the film's romantic theme, abandons these Orientalist signifiers entirely for a standard Broadway ballad structure, and the lyrics ironically suggest that there's a better "world" devoid of the inferior "Other" (Baber & Spickard, 2015). While the "Other" provides exotic flavor, Western musical hegemony persists as the film's dominant musical language.
