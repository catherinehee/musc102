Aladdin

1992

Phase I: The Renaissance Era

Overview: Orientalism in Action

Aladdin (1992) deploys Orientalist musical tactics that define the Arab world as savage and inferior. The film exemplifies Phase I of Disney's approach: exclusion, stereotyping, and the deployment of music as superficial signification rather than culturally informed representation.

"Arabian Nights": The White Gaze

"Where they cut off your ear / If they don't like your face / It's barbaric, but hey, it's home."

— Original lyrics to "Arabian Nights" (later revised after protests)

The opening song, "Arabian Nights," initially described the Middle Eastern setting as a place of barbarism and violence. While protests forced a lyrical revision, the retained line, "It's barbaric, but hey, it's home," signaled the film's ideological stance. The lyrics frame the setting through a "white gaze," geographically and morally positioning American culture as superior to the depicted "Other."

Key Problems with the Lyrics

  • Frames Middle Eastern culture through Western superiority complex
  • Uses violence and "barbarism" as defining characteristics
  • Even after revision, maintains judgmental stance
  • Sets tone for entire film's cultural representation

Musical Orientalism: "Aural Shorthand"

Composers Alan Menken and Howard Ashman utilized what scholars call "aural shorthand" — recognizable markers of exoticism like the augmented second interval — rather than authentic Arabic music. This interval is not specific to Arab culture but is a generalized Western signifier for foreignness.

"This strategy reveals a crucial power dynamic where European fantasies, not Arab people, define Middle Eastern identity in the film."

— Critical analysis of Aladdin's musical choices

The exclusion of Arab musicians from the creative process underscores the extractive approach of this era. The music tells us nothing about actual Arab musical traditions — it only tells us about Western stereotypes of what "exotic" should sound like.

"A Whole New World": Western Musical Hegemony

The film's romantic centerpiece, "A Whole New World," abandons these "exotic" signifiers entirely for a standard Broadway ballad structure. This represents a crucial ideological move: while the "Other" provides exotic flavor for establishing shots and secondary characters, Western musical hegemony must structure the sonic core of the narrative.

What This Musical Choice Reveals

  • Romance (universal emotion) requires Western musical language
  • "Exotic" music relegated to setting/atmosphere only
  • Emotional depth expressed through familiar Broadway conventions
  • Implies non-Western music inadequate for conveying "real" emotion

The Exclusion Problem

Perhaps most tellingly, no Arab musicians were involved in the creative process. The entire sonic representation of Arab culture was created by Western composers working from stereotypes and cinematic conventions, not from engagement with actual Arabic musical traditions.

This exclusion is not merely an oversight — it's fundamental to how Orientalism operates. Said's framework explains that the Orient is constructed for Western consumption, not by the people who actually inhabit those spaces.

Cultural Impact & Legacy

Aladdin became one of Disney's most successful films, grossing over $500 million worldwide and introducing millions of children to a "Middle East" that existed only in Western imagination. The film's musical stereotypes became the default sonic representation of Arab culture for an entire generation.

This case demonstrates Phase I logic clearly: cultural representation is entirely subordinated to Western commercial interests, with no input from the represented communities. The music serves colonial fantasy, not cultural understanding.
